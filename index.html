Introduction and Team List

Engineers use Calculus every day. From calculating thrust to modelling airflow around a rocket, Calculus is the starting point of many engineering endeavors. Engineering can be thought of as practical Physics, and Calculus is the language of Physics. So, engineers of all fields must employ the tools and concepts of Calculus for much of their work. This is no different for Aerospace Engineers.

Aerospace Engineers are concerned with the dynamics of vehicles in air and space. One prominent field of Aerospace Engineering is rocketry. Rocketry encompasses many disciplines, and there are too many applications of Calculus to give a comprehensive list in a small project. So, we have decided to focus on two applications of Calculus that are particularly useful in rocketry.

These two applications are the rocket equation and the Navier-Stokes Equations. The rocket equation relates the starting and ending mass of a rocket with the gain in velocity. This is important if you need to achieve a specific velocity and you only have the mass of the rocket, the mass of the fuel, and the velocity of the exhaust particles to work with. In this presentation, we derive the rocket equation from a and important principle in physics call the conservation of momentum.

The Navier-Stokes Equations are an important tool in fluid dynamics. They relate the pressure, viscosity, and momentum to the rate at which fluid flows in all directions. Using these equations, one can model how a fluid will flow given certain initial conditions. This is important for modelling how air will flow around the cone of a rocket, or how exhaust from the rocket engine will move inside the cone. In this presentation, we solve a simple fluid dynamics problem using the Navier-Stokes Equations.

Rocket Equation

Figuring out how much velocity a rocket gains after burning some of its fuel is an important thing for rocket engineers to figure out. To do this, they have developed an equation known as the rocket equation. The rocket equation equates a rocket’s beginning and ending mass to the change in velocity after a burn. To derive it, scientists used Calculus and the conservation of momentum.

Conservation of Momentum

The conservation of momentum is a law of physics that says that the total momentum of a closed system remains constant as the system evolves in time.

\[momentum:\ p=mv\] \[\sum{p_{before}}=\sum{p_{after}}\]

In the first equation, \(p\) is momentum, \(m\) is mass, and \(v\) is the velocity of the mass. The second equation says that the sum of the momentum of every part of the system before an event is equal to the sum of the momentum of the parts after the event. Conservation of momentum is one of the most important equations in physics. It is fundamental to many equations and laws. By examining a rocket before and after burning fuel and expelling exhaust propellent, we can use this principle to define the rocket equation.

Before the burn, the rocket’s momentum is p=mv_0. During the burn, the rocket expels tiny particles of exhaust propellent (\(dm\)) which are tiny pieces of the total mass of the rocket. Every little particle has a velocity of \(v_e\). Even though the exhaust velocity is constant, the velocity of each particle from the perspective of the system is \(v_0-v_e\). This is because the momentum of the system is dependent on an inertial reference frame. Basically, this is what a person standing still on the ground would measure the velocities of the parts in the system to be.

After expelling the particle, the rocket’s mass is \(m-dm\), and the rocket’s new velocity is \(v_0+dv\), where \(dv\) is a tiny change in velocity due to Newton’s Second Law (which states that every action has an equal and opposite reaction). The total momentum of the rocket before the burn must be equal to the total momentum after the burn. So, the equation of the total momentum is as follows:

\[{\rm mv}_0=dm\left(v_0-v_e\right)+\left(m-dm\right)\left(v_0+dv\right)\]

Rearrange and cancel terms to simplify the equation.

\[{\rm mv}_0=v_0dm-v_edm+{\rm mv}_0+mdv-v_0dm-dmdv\] \[0=-v_edm+mdv-dmdv\]

The quantity \(dmdv\) is the tiny change in the rocket’s mass times the tiny change in velocity, which will be a very tiny quantity. So tiny, in fact, that its impact on the total momentum is negligible. It can be removed without consequence. This leaves us with the following.

\[v_edm=mdv\]

From here, we want to isolate the mass and velocity variables. \(v_e\) is a constant and is independent of the velocity of the rocket. So, it can stay where it is.

\[dv=v_e\frac{1}{m}dm\]

The rocket will continuously expel exhaust particles, which will continuously add tiny changes to the velocity. The sum of all the changes in velocity will equal the sum of all the changes in mass times the exhaust velocity of each particle.

\[\sum_{i=0}^{n}{\rm dv}_i=v_e\sum_{i=0}^{n}{{f(m)}_idm}_i\] \[f(m)=\frac{1}{m}\]

Since the changes are continuous and the change in mass and velocity are both very tiny (almost zero), we can use integrals to relate the total change in mass to the total change in velocity. The boundaries of integration of the velocity side will be from the rocket’s initial velocity to a final velocity, and the boundaries of integration of the mass side will be from the rocket’s initial mass to the final mass after the fuel is spent.

\[\int_{v_0}^{v_f}dv=v_e\int_{m_0}^{m_f}{\frac{1}{m}dm}\] \[\left[v\right]_{v_0}^{v_f}=v_e\left[\ln{\left|m\right|}\right]_{m_0}^{m_f}\] \[v_f-v_0=v_e\left(\ln{\left|m_f\right|}-\ln{\left|m_0\right|}\right)\]

We can use the subtraction law of logarithms (\(\ln{a}-\ln{b}=\ln{\frac{a}{b}}\)) to simplify the equation. The delta-v symbol (\(∆v\)) can replace v_f-v_0. We can also get rid of the absolute value since mass is always positive.

\[∆v=v_e\ln{\frac{m_f}{m_0}}\]

This is the rocket equation. It describes the amount of velocity a rocket will gain by losing a certain amount of fuel that is expelled from the rocket at a certain exhaust velocity. This equation is indispensable for rocket engineers.

Introduction to Vector Fields and Multivariable Derivatives

Liquids are fluids. Gases are fluids, too. One of the things Aerospace Engineers want to be able to do is model fluid flow (like rocket exhaust coming out of a nozzle or water flowing in a pipe). One of the most powerful tools engineers have for modelling fluid flow is the Navier-Stokes equations. To understand these equations, though, it is necessary to define a few concepts that are a bit ahead of Calculus 1 (namely vectors, vector fields, and multivariable derivatives).

Vectors and Vector Fields

A vector is a mathematical object that has both a direction and a magnitude. Vectors can be two-dimensional, three-dimensional, or higher. For simplicity, only two-dimensional vectors are discussed here. Vectors are often represented with arrows.

Quantities that are vectors (so called vector quantities) are usually indicated with a bar or arrow above them (like \(\bar{u}\) or \(\vec{u}\)). Vectors are made up of x-direction and y-direction components and can be expressed in different ways, all of which are equally valid.

\[\bar{u}=1\hat{i}+2\hat{j}, \bar{u}=\langle1,2\rangle, \bar{u}=\left[\begin{matrix}1\\2\\\end{matrix}\right]\]

These are a few ways of expressing a vector that has one unit in the x- direction and two units in the y- direction. \(\hat{i}\) and \(\hat{j}\) are called hat vectors and represent the most fundamental step in a particular direction. \(\hat{i}\) represents the x-direction. \(\hat{j}\) represents the y-direction. The magnitude of the vector is the distance from the starting point to the ending point of the vector. This is found using the distance equation, \(d=\sqrt{\left(x_1-x_0\right)^2+\left(y_1-y_0\right)^2}\). The vector has an angle (\(\theta\)) associated with it as well. Vector angles are generally measured counterclockwise from the x-axis (pointing right).

The component values of the vector can be from one point to another point, or they can be from the origin to the given coordinate.

Even though the two vectors pictured above have different starting and ending points, they are the same vector. Vectors that have the same angle and magnitude are identical, regardless of where they start from. For this reason, vectors are graphed from the origin, (0,0), to the coordinate (x-component, y-component). This makes finding the magnitude and angle of the vector more straightforward.

By changing the components of the vector, you cause it to shorten or elongate and rotate about the origin. Negative vectors (i.e., \(-\bar{u}\)) are vectors that have the same magnitude as their positive versions but point in the opposite direction.

In the above picture, vectors \(\bar{u}\) and \(-\bar{u}\) have the same magnitude of \(\sqrt{\left(1^2+1^2\right)}=\sqrt{\left(\left(-1\right)^2+\left(-1\right)^2\right)}=\sqrt{\left(2\right)}\) but are pointing in opposite directions. Vector \(\bar{v}\), on the other hand, has a magnitude of \(\sqrt{\left(\frac{1}{4}\right)^2+\left(\frac{7}{4}\right)^2}=\frac{5\sqrt2}{4}\) and is pointing in a direction different from either of the other vectors.

Vector fields

A vector field is, basically, a graph that assigns a different vector to every coordinate of the graph.

Choosing a coordinate in a vector field is like dropping a tiny particle at that x, y coordinate. The vector at that coordinate tells you how that particle will move from where it is. It will move in a certain direction for a certain distance.

Vector fields are defined the same way as vectors, but each component can be a function of multiple variables. Here is an example:

\[\vec{V}=y\hat{i}\ +(y\ -\ x)\hat{j}\]

This vector field says that for any coordinate (x, y), the x-direction will be y units long, and the y-direction will be y - x units long. So, at the coordinate (1, 2) the vector will be 2,1, and a particle would move up 2 units and to the right 1 unit.

At coordinate (3, 1) the vector is 1,-2, and a particle would move down 2 units and to the right 1 unit.

Since vector fields contain functions of multiple variables, it is helpful to understand how to calculate rates of change with multiple variables.

Derivatives of Multivariable Functions

The rules for derivatives for multivariable functions are almost identical to single variable derivatives, but with a few twists. When working with functions (f) of a single variable, the way you indicate a derivative is \(\frac{df}{dx}\). When there are multiple variables, however, things are a bit more complicated. When finding the derivative with respect to only one variable, every other variable is treated like a constant, and you use \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} to indicate the derivative. The symbol \(\partial\) is called a partial derivative. It means that only the designated variable will affect the derivative. Here is an example:

\[f\left(x,y\right)=x^2y^2+x\sin{y}+y^3\] \[\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}=2xy^2+\sin{y}\]

Only the terms that contain x are differentiated. Any functions of y (like y^2 or \sin{y}) are treated as if they are constants and either not changed or eliminated (because \frac{d}{dx}k=0, where k is a constant). This is the essence of multivariable derivatives.

Gradient and Divergence

Vector fields are composed of components that are themselves functions of multiple variables. Often, it is necessary to understand how a vector field is changing in each of its directions. To do this, one must find the partial derivatives with respect to each of the directions of each component. If the vector field is \(\vec{V}=u(x,y)\hat{i}+v(x,y)\hat{j}\), and \(u\left(x,y\right)=x^2y\), \(v\left(x,y\right)=\frac{y}{x}\), then the change in the components of each direction are \(\vec{V}\prime=\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\hat{i}+\frac{\partial v}{\partial y}\hat{j}=2xy\hat{i}+\frac{1}{x}\hat{j}\). Each component is differentiated with respect to the direction (x or y) that it affects.

There is a special name for the derivative of a vector field: the gradient. Its symbol is called the “del” or “nabla” symbol (\nabla), which is referred to as an operator (an operator is like a function that acts on inputs; addition, +, is an operator that adds two numbers together; a derivative, \frac{d}{dx}, is an operator that differentiates a function with respect to a variable, etc.). The del operator can be used in different ways. Another property of vector fields that uses the del operator is divergence.

Divergence

Divergence, written \(\nabla\bullet\vec{V}\), is different from a gradient. It is the sum of the partial derivatives of the components of the vector field.

\[divergence:\ \nabla\bullet V=\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial v}{\partial y}\] \[gradient:\ \nabla V=\langle\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}, \frac{\partial v}{\partial y}\rangle\]

The divergence is a scalar quantity (represented by a number that has only one component), and the gradient is a vector quantity. The gradient tells you how a vector field is changing along each axis. Divergence tells you how much flow is coming in or going out of a tiny region of the vector field. If arrows represent vectors, then flow would be the concentration of arrows in an area. If you envision a circle around a part of the vector field, the divergence is a measure of how many arrows are coming into or going out of that tiny region.

If more arrows are going into the region than coming out, the divergence at that point is negative. An example of this is a sink.

If more arrows are going away from the circle than coming in, the divergence is positive. An example of this is a source.

If the divergence is zero, the vector field is non-compressible. That means that the same number of arrows are entering a region as leaving.

Navier-Stokes Equations

The Navier-Stokes Equations (NS equations), named after the men who developed them (Claude-Louis Navier and George Gabriel Stokes), are a set of partial differential equations that relate the viscosity, momentum, pressure, and flow rate of a fluid together. There are two forms of the equations: one for non-compressible fluids (i.e., the divergence of the vector field is zero), and one for compressible fluids. We will only use the non-compressible, two-dimensional form of the equation. There are three equations for this form.

\[\nabla\bullet\vec{F}=\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial v}{\partial y}=0\] \[\rho\left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}+u\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}+v\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}\right)=\rho{\bar{g}}_x-\frac{\partial P}{\partial x}+\mu\left(\frac{\partial^2u}{\partial x^2}+\frac{\partial^2u}{\partial y^2}\right)\] \[\rho\left(\frac{\partial v}{\partial t}+u\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}+v\frac{\partial v}{\partial y}\right)=\rho{\bar{g}}_y-\frac{\partial P}{\partial y}+\mu\left(\frac{\partial^2v}{\partial x^2}+\frac{\partial^2v}{\partial y^2}\right)\]

The vector field, \(\vec{F}\), is composed of \(u\) (the x-direction component) and \(v\) (the y-direction component). Both \(u\) and \(v\) are functions of \(x\) and \(y\). The first equation says that the divergence is zero. This is known as the conservation of mass equation. The second and third equations relate the momentum (\(\rho\)), viscosity (\(mu\)), gravity field (\(\bar{g}\)), pressure (\(P\)), and vector field components (\(u\) and \(v\)) together. There is one equation for each direction of the vector field.

Since the NS equations are difficult partial differential equations, solutions to them are approximations, and it is unknown if there are solutions for every three-dimensional vector field. However, there are always solutions for two-dimensional vector fields. It is possible to model rocket exhaust using the NS equations, but that is immensely difficult, and the math required is far outside the scope of Calculus 1. There are less complicated solutions that simplify to single variable vector fields that can be solved using the tools in Calculus 1.

French Physicist Jean Léonard Marie Poiseuille discovered such a solution. It involves a viscous fluid flowing between two fixed plates. Here is a version of that solution.

Problem

What is the maximum flow rate of a non-compressible, laminar liquid flowing steadily in only one direction between two fixed plates that are one centimeter apart. The liquid has a viscosity of 0.001 Pascal Seconds (Pa∙S) and is driven with a constant pressure of 300 Pascals. The system is on a rocket in orbit. So, gravity is negligible.

Solution

With the information given to us in the description, we can cancel out serval terms. The fluid is flowing in only one direction (the \(x\)-direction), the vector field will be in the form \(\vec{F}=u\hat{i}+0\hat{j}\). Since \(v=0\), any term that has a \(v\) in it is also zero, and the entire second equation will also be zero. The fluid is flowing steadily. That means that the change in the x-direction of the field (\(\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}\)) is zero. The entire gravity vector is zero. We can also figure out what \(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\) is also because of the conservation of mass equation.

\[\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial v}{\partial y}=0\] \[v=0\] \[\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}+0=0\] \[\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}=0\]

This leaves us with the following.

\[\rho\left(0+u\left(0\right)+\left(0\right)\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}\right)=\rho\left(0\right)-\frac{\partial P}{\partial x}+\mu\left(0+\frac{\partial^2u}{\partial y^2}\right)\] \[0=-\frac{\partial P}{\partial x}+\mu\frac{\partial^2u}{\partial y^2}\] \[\frac{\partial^2u}{\partial y^2}=\frac{1}{\mu}\frac{\partial P}{\partial x}\]

The pressure only has an x-direction component. So, pressure is a function of x only. By examining the picture of the problem, we can tell that the horizontal length of every vector in the field is the same from left to right (along the x-axis), but the horizontal length of the vectors is different from top to bottom. As x changes, u stays the same, but as y changes, u changes as well. This means that u is a function of y only. Since both the pressure and horizontal component of the vector field are functions of a single variable, we can rewrite their partial derivatives as single variable derivatives.

\[\frac{d^2u}{dy^2}=\frac{1}{\mu}\frac{dP}{dx}\]

Fluids flow from higher pressure to lower pressure. This means that as the x increases, the pressure decreases at a constant rate. The pressure vector is \(\bar{P}=-300x\hat{i}+0\hat{j}\). Thus, \(\frac{dP}{dx}=-300\). The viscosity is 0.001 Pa∙S.

\[\frac{d^2u}{dy^2}=-\frac{300}{0.001}=-300,000\]

We can solve for u by double integrating the second derivative.

\[\frac{du}{dy}=\int{-300,000dy=-300,000y+C_1}\] \[u=\int{\left(-300,000y+C_1\right)dy=-150,000y^2+}C_1y+C_2\]

We can solve for \(C_1\) and \(C_2\) by examining the boundary conditions of the system. At the plates, the vector is zero. This is called the no slip condition. In the middle of the plates, the flow of the fluid is at its maximum. If we set the middle of the plates as \(y=0\), then at \(y=0.01\) and \(y=-0.01\) (units have to be in meters), \(u\) would equal 0. This means that \(u\left(0.01\right)=u\left(-0.01\right)=0\). We can use this fact to determine what the constants of integration are.

\[-150,000\left(0.01\right)^2+C_1\left(0.01\right)+C_2=-150,000\left(-0.01\right)^2+C_1\left(-0.01\right)+C_2\] \[-15+C_1\left(0.01\right)+C_2=-15-C_1\left(0.01\right)+C_2\] \[0.02C_1=0\] \[C_1=0\]

Putting this value of C_1 back into one of the equations will let us solve \(C_2\).

\[-150,000\left(0.0001\right)+C_2=0\] \[C_2=150,000\left(0.0001\right)\]

The final value of u is this:

\[u=-150,000y^2+150,000\left(0.0001\right)\] \[u=150,000\left(0.0001-y^2\right)\]

The flow rate is at its maximum in the middle of the plates (where y = 0).

\[u_{max}=150,000\left(0.0001-0\right)\] \[\fbox{$u_{max}=15\frac{m}{s}$}\]

Conclusion

Calculus is a power tool for Aerospace Engineers. The Navier-Stokes Equations help model fluid flow in dynamic systems. The rocket equation lets engineers know the velocity increase from burning an arbitrary amount of fuel. There are many more wonderful applications of Calculus in Aerospace Engineering. It is an ever-changing, ever-growing field. And you can be sure that however the field grows, Calculus will be the fundamental tool that facilitates its developments.
